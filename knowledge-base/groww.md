Word of the Day
Rebalancing


It means adjusting your portfolio to maintain the desired allocation of assets like stocks, bonds or gold

The value of assets can change as the market changes. This can cause an investor’s portfolio to shift from its intended asset profile.

Investors buy/sell assets to bring their portfolio back to their desired risk level.

This is rebalancing.

Rebalancing is done to help control risk and keep the portfolio aligned with long-term financial goals.

Example: say stocks make up 80% of an investor's portfolio. The investor can return to their desired 60% stocks and 40% debt allocation by either selling some stocks or investing more in debt.

Now, let’s look at the factors that cause the price of crude to go up or down.

A few countries supply the crude oil while most countries consume it.

Supply and demand obviously affect the price. Some of the largest supplying countries actually try to control the supply too. They do this so that there is not too much oil. Too much supply causes the prices to fall which is not good for them.

They are often accused of artificially keeping the prices high. This is contested since when the price gets too high, other competing nations increase their supply in order to be able to earn more.

Many oil consuming countries try to reduce their crude oil imports by using other sources of energy (nuclear, coal, hydro, etc to make more electricity to reduce reliance on crude oil products).

Since only a few countries produce all the oil, any regional or geopolitical disturbances in the regions also affect supply leading to higher prices.

Distance between the supplying side and the consuming side also affects the price. The nearer, the lower the price.

Local factors are also at play.

And of course, demand from economic factors like a country with a growing middle class, etc also increases demand, increasing prices.

Featured Question
Q. “In India, who decides the price of fuel like Petrol, Diesel and LPG and what factors contribute to arrive at the price? What is the tax structure on such fuel?”
Officially, the prices of these fuels are deregulated.
This means that their price changes based on whatever the price of their raw material is.

So in theory, when the price of crude oil rises, the price of petrol and diesel should go up. Similarly, when the price of natural gas goes up, the LPG price also should go up.

The government maintains a sort of control over this. They try to cushion the price change shock.

So, it starts with the cost of the raw material (crude oil or natural gas).

To add to that, the cost of transporting the material, refining/processing it, and then locally storing and transporting it.

There’s a dealer profit component in this too.

And finally, there’s tax.

The central government adds excise duty to the price. This is a fixed price (not percentage).

The state government adds a VAT on its level. This is why different states have different prices. Some places also have a cess added to this.
